  • Castle Point
  • The Castle Point in Daytime. Castle Point is a great place for the proposals.
  • Castle Point is the highest point in Hoboken. The name is a corruption of "Castille Point", due to its supposed resemblance to the Castilian coast in Spain. To early navigators, the high serpentine crag jutting over the river reminded them of a miniature Rock of Gibraltar. The land was bought at public auction in 1804 by Col. John Stevens, who built his estate there. After his death, his son Edwin Augustus Stevens took responsibility of the estate and in 1853 built a 40-room mansion, the "Stevens Castle", on land adjacent to the point.
  • The Castle Point in Nighttime. The lights in New York City shine up the whole world. Great place to be in when you are in peace.

  • Hudson River
  • This iconic river of New York City, perhaps more well known for its stereotype of being polluted, the Hudson River, part of the Erie Canal, stands as the border between New Jersey and New York. By other people, it is known as Ca-ho-ha-ta-te-a by the Iroquois and Muh-he-kun-ne-tuk by the Mohican,, it was named after the explorer Henry Hudson, and it is the largest river in the state of New York.





  • Humphrey Hall
  • One of seven residential halls on the Stevens campus, along with Hayden Hall, Palmer Hall, Jonas Hall, Lore-El Center, Castle Point Hall, and Davis Hall, Humphreys is one of the two used for pre-college students, and is located near the Howe center on the upper side of campus. Having a capacity of 150 students, and the iconic beach volleyball setup on its lawn, regardless of if one is living there, Humphrey’s Hall is a place to go.

  • The Torch Bearer
  • Right in the middle of campus, the torch bearer is a statue of a naked man grabbing a torch on his horse from a fallen runner and preparing to ride off. It is surrounded by garden and is one of the more nature driven features of the university. The statue was sculpted by American Anna Hyatt Huntington and it is the largest cast aluminum sculpture in the world, and is located next to the Samuel C. Williams Library.
